Every participant who completes the AI judging pipeline gets a dedicated report. The report is your primary workspace for reviewing AI analysis, preparing live Q&A questions, and entering your final Jury Score. This page explains what each section shows and how to get the most out of it.

## Report header

The report header appears at the top of every tab and gives you a quick orientation before you dive into the details.

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| Participant name | The name submitted with the application. |
| Project name | The startup or project name submitted by the participant. |
| AI Total Score | The AI's overall score for this participant on a 0.0–10.0 scale. This is advisory — the Jury Score you enter determines the leaderboard ranking. |
| Status badge | The current participant status (`AI Report Ready`, `In Review`, `Scored`, etc.). |
| Navigation | A link to go back to the participant list. In the current version there is no direct previous/next navigation between participants — return to the list to open another participant. |

After the leaderboard is generated, the header also displays **Finalized · [date]** and all inputs become read-only.

## Tab 1 — Executive Summary

The Executive Summary gives you a complete picture of the participant's project before you begin human scoring. It is the default tab when you open a report from anywhere other than the Jury Review list.

### Pitch deck carousel

The top of the tab displays a slide-by-slide carousel of the submitted pitch deck. Slides with missing or weakly-covered content blocks are flagged so you can identify gaps at a glance.

### Project description

The participant's own written description of their project, submitted as part of their application. This field is read-only — it represents the participant's voice and is shown exactly as submitted.

### AI Consolidated Summary

A narrative paragraph generated by the Summarizer after aggregating all judge outputs. This is not a repeat of the scores — it is a coherent written overview of the project's strengths and weaknesses as seen across the full judge panel.

### AI Total Score

The overall score on the 0.0–10.0 scale, identical to the value in the report header. The Jury Score you enter in the Jury Preparation tab uses the same scale.

### P1–P6 score overview

A compact overview of the AI score for each evaluation dimension (P1–P6 for Pitch Competition), each labeled with its confidence level: **low**, **medium**, or **high**. Confidence reflects how clearly the submitted materials supported a definitive judgment on that dimension.

A scoring legend is shown alongside the overview to explain the scale and confidence labels.

### Deck completeness signals

Six flags indicating whether key content blocks are present and well-covered in the pitch deck:

- Problem statement
- Solution explanation
- Business model / GTM
- Roadmap / feasibility
- Team evidence
- Market source / evidence

<Note>
  Deck completeness signals are not a fact-check. They do not verify whether the participant's claims are accurate — they only flag whether a given block appears to be missing or weakly addressed in the deck. Use them as a guide for which areas to probe during live Q&A.
</Note>

### Why can pass / Why can fail / What must be confirmed live

Three short sections generated by the Summarizer, each written from the perspective of the full judge panel:

- **Why can pass** — the strongest arguments in favor of this project.
- **Why can fail** — the most significant concerns or risks.
- **What must be confirmed live** — specific claims or gaps that require a direct answer from the participant during Q&A.

### Strengths and weaknesses

A structured list of the project's key strengths and weaknesses, derived from the judge panel's analysis. Each item is marked with a consensus indicator:

| Marker | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Consensus | Most or all judges agreed on this point. |
| Split | Judges had noticeably different views. |
| Conflict | Judges disagreed significantly — treat with extra scrutiny. |

## Tab 2 — Jury Preparation

Jury Preparation is the main scoring entry point. When you open a report from the Jury Review list, this tab opens by default.

### Scoring matrix

A read-only table showing the AI score for each evaluation dimension (P1–P6). Use these scores as a reference baseline when entering your own Jury Scores below. The AI scores in this matrix cannot be edited.

### Questions for participants

A list of suggested questions for your live Q&A session, organized into three priority groups:

| Group | When to use |
|---|---|
| Critical | High-priority questions about unresolved gaps or risks identified by the judges. Ask these first. |
| Important | Secondary questions that add useful context but are not blockers. |
| Optional | Additional questions worth asking if time allows. |

Each question includes:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Question text | The question itself, generated by the AI judge panel. Read-only. |
| Linked criterion | Which evaluation dimension (P1–P6) this question relates to. |
| Why it matters | A brief explanation of why this question is significant for the evaluation. |
| Source judge | Which AI judge or the Summarizer generated this question. |
| Answer notes | An optional free-text field where you can record the participant's response or your observations. |
| Answered / Not answered | An optional toggle to track whether you asked this question during Q&A. |

<Note>
  None of the question fields — including answer notes and the answered toggle — are required to submit a Jury Score. They exist to support your preparation and create an audit trail, but leaving them blank does not block you from voting.
</Note>

## Tab 3 — Scores

The Scores tab explains the methodology behind the AI scores and shows the full breakdown of how the final AI Total Score was calculated for this participant.

### Methodology

A plain-language explanation of the scoring formulas used:

- **AI Criterion Score** — the weighted average of all judges' scores on a given dimension, adjusted for each judge's confidence level.
- **AI Total Score** — the weighted sum of all dimension scores using the criteria weights you set in the wizard.

Each judge has a role for each dimension — **Primary**, **Secondary**, or **Advisory** — which determines their weight in the weighted average. Confidence adjustments are applied on top: a high-confidence score counts more than a low-confidence score on the same dimension.

### AI Score Formation table

A per-dimension breakdown showing exactly how the AI Total Score was built for this participant:

| Column | Description |
|---|---|
| Dimension code | P1, P2, P3, P4, P5, or P6. |
| Dimension name | The full name of the evaluation criterion. |
| AI score | The confidence-adjusted score for this dimension (0.0–10.0). |
| Weight | The criterion weight you configured in the wizard. |
| Weighted contribution | AI score × weight, showing this dimension's contribution to the total. |
| Confidence | The overall confidence level for this dimension (low / medium / high). |

The bottom row shows the AI Total Score as the sum of all weighted contributions.

### Judge Contribution Matrix

A grid showing every judge's score and role for every dimension they cover:

- Columns represent evaluation dimensions (P1–P6).
- Rows represent individual judges.
- Each cell shows the judge's role (Primary / Secondary / Advisory) and their score.
- Cells with large score differences between judges are highlighted to draw your attention.

The spread label for each dimension appears below or alongside the matrix:

| Label | Score spread between judges |
|---|---|
| CONSENSUS | Less than 1.5 points |
| SPLIT | 1.5 to 2.99 points |
| CONFLICT | 3.0 points or more |

High spread on a dimension means the AI judges disagreed significantly — treat that dimension's score with more scrutiny and use the live Q&A to investigate.

### P1–P6 Detailed Breakdown

An accordion section per dimension containing the full reasoning behind that dimension's score:

- AI score, weight, and confidence for this dimension
- Short conclusion summarizing the overall finding
- What supports the score (evidence and signals that raised it)
- What reduces the score (evidence and signals that lowered it)
- Score consolidation (how the judges' inputs were combined)
- Move-up signals (what would push this score higher)
- Move-down signals (what would push this score lower)

## Tab 4 — Judges

The Judges tab shows the individual output of each AI judge as an accordion card. For a Pitch Competition project, there are six judge cards — one for each judge in the panel.

Each card contains:

| Section | Description |
|---|---|
| Primary concern | The main issue or risk this judge identified for the project. |
| Would ask live | The single most important question this judge would ask the team in a live session. |
| Scores per dimension | A table of the scores this judge gave for each dimension they cover, along with their role (Primary / Secondary / Advisory). |
| Top signals | The key observations from this judge's analysis, each tagged with an origin code linking the signal back to the source material. |
| Raw reasoning | A toggle that reveals the judge's full unfiltered reasoning output. Useful when you want to understand exactly how a judge arrived at a score. |

---

## Tab 5 — Reviewer

The Reviewer tab duplicates the scoring matrix from Tab 2 (Jury Preparation). It is designed to give you quick access to the AI scores for reference while you are in the middle of live Q&A or entering scores in another tool — without having to navigate away from where you are working.

The matrix is read-only and stays in sync with the Jury Preparation tab.

---

## Report lifecycle

| Stage | What you can do |
|---|---|
| Before judging | No report exists. You can view participant details and submitted materials, but there is no AI analysis yet. |
| After judging | The full report is available across all five tabs. You can enter and submit a Jury Score. |
| After the leaderboard | The report is read-only. All inputs are locked. The header shows **Finalized · [date]**. The report remains accessible for reference and audit purposes. |
